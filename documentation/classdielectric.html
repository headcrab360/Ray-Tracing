Dielectric(glass) sufaces diffuse class.

Snell's Law implementation On the refracted side of the surface there is a refracted ray R' and a normal n', and there exists an angle, theta', between them. We can split R' into the parts of the ray that are perpendicular to n' and parallel to n': R'=R'_perp + R'_parallel R'_perp = eta/eta_prime(R+cos theta n) R'_parallel = sqrt(1−|R'_perp|^2 * n) cos theta: a*b=|a||b|cos theta If we restrict a and b to be unit vectors: a*b=cos theta. We can now rewrite R'_perp in terms of known quantities: R'_perp = eta/eta_prime(R+(−R⋅n)n)

Total Internal Reflection sin_theta_prime = eta/eta_prime * sin theta If the ray is inside glass and outside is air (eta=1.5 and eta_prime=1.0): sin_eta_prime = 1.5/1.0 * sin theta The value of sin_eta_prime cannot be greater than 1. So, if, 1.5/1.0 * sin theta > 1.0 Here all the light is reflected, and because in practice that is usually inside solid objects, it is called 'total internal reflection'. This is why sometimes the water-air boundary acts as a perfect mirror when you are submerged. We can solve for sin_theta using the trigonometric qualities: sin theta = sqrt(1−cos^2(theta)) and cos_theta = R*n
